International Space Center

ISS Historical Timeline

    President Reagan directs NASA to build the ISS (January 25, 1984)

  • President Ronald Reagan’s State of the Union Address directs NASA to build an international space station within the next 10 years.

    • First ISS Segment Launches (November 20th, 1998)

  • The first segment of the ISS launches: a Russian proton rocket named Zarya (“sunrise”).

    • First U.S.-built component launches (December 4, 1998)

  • Unity, the first U.S.-built component of the International Space Station launches—the first Space Shuttle mission dedicated to assembly of the station.

    • U.S. Lab Module Recognized as Newest U.S. National Laboratory (2005)

  • Congress designates the U.S. portion of the ISS as the nation’s newest national laboratory to maximize its use for other U.S. government agencies and for academic and private institutions.

    • European Lab / Japanese Lab Joins the ISS (2008)

  • The European Space Agency’s Columbus Laboratory becomes part of the station.
    The first Japanese Kibo laboratory module becomes part of the station.

    • ISS 10-Year Anniversary(November 2, 2010)

  • The International Space Station celebrates its 10-year anniversary of continuous human occupation. Since Expedition 1 in the fall of 2000, 202 people had visited the station.

    • First CASIS Research Flight (2013)

  • Proteins can be grown as crystals in space with nearly perfect three-dimensional structures useful for the development of new drugs. CASIS’ PCG series of flights began in 2013, allowing researchers to utilize the unique environment of the ISS National Lab.

About ISS

›   The International Space Station (ISS) is a space station, or a habitable artificial satellite, in low Earth orbit. Its first component launched into orbit in 1998, the last pressurised module was fitted in 2011, and the station is expected to be used until 2028.
›   Zarya, the first ISS module, was launched by a Proton rocket on 20 November 1998. The STS-88 shuttle mission followed two weeks after Zarya was launched, bringing Unity, the first of three node modules, and connecting it to Zarya.
›   This means that the owners of the Space Station - the United States, Russia, the European Partner, Japan and Canada - are legally responsible for the respective elements they provide. The European States are being treated as one homogenous entity, called the European Partner on the Space Station.
›   Every so often, you can see the ISS in your night sky. To us on Earth, it looks like a bright star moving quickly above the horizon. Then, just as suddenly as it appears, it disappears. ... Visible to the naked eye, the station is best seen at dawn and dusk, and is the third brightest object in the sky.

Notable Milestones

The ISS has had several notable milestones over the years, when it comes to crews:

›   Most consecutive days in space by an American: 340 days, which happened when Scott Kelly took part in a one-year mission to  the International Space Station in 2015-16 (along with Russian cosmonaut Mikhail Kornienko).  The space agencies did a comprehensive suite of experiments on the astronauts, including a "twin study" with Kelly and  his Earth-bound former astronaut twin,Mark.  NASA has expressed interest in more long-duration missions, although none have yet been announced.
›   Longest single spaceflight by a woman: 289 days, during American astronaut Peggy Whitson's 2016-17 mission aboard the space station.
›  Most total time spent in space by a woman: Again, that's Peggy Whitson, who racked up most of her 665 days in space on the ISS.
›  Biggest space gathering: 13 people, during NASA's STS-127 shuttle mission aboard Endeavour in 2009.  (It's been tied a few times during later missions.)
›  Longest single spacewalk: 8 hours and 56 minutes during STS-102, for an ISS construction mission in 2001.  NASA astronauts Jim Voss and Susan Helms participated.
›  Longest Russian spacewalk: 8 hours and 13 minutes during Expedition 54, to repair an ISS antenna.  Russian astronauts Alexander Misurkin and Anton Shkaplerov participated.
